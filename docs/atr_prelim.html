Key Results

  • Control Treatment Dynamics:
    • Demonstrated a higher abundance of labile cation-organic associations.
    • Extraction induced a highly consistent shift in the carboxylate environment, characterized by a decrease in the \(COO^-\) asymmetric stretching region and a corresponding increase in the \(COO^-\) symmetric stretching region.
  • Rock Powder Treatment Stability:
    • Significantly reduced the sensitivity of the carboxylate environment to chemical extraction.
    • Indicates the formation of more stable, less extractable organo-mineral complexes.
  • Mineral Matrix Preservation:
    • The silicate region remained largely stable across extractions, confirming that the underlying mineral matrix was conserved.

Interpretations & Next Steps

Primary Interpretation: Enhanced Organo-Mineral Passivation

If the rock powder successfully weathered and released structural cations (\(\text{Ca}^{2+}\), \(\text{Mg}^{2+}\), \(\text{Fe}^{3+}\), or \(\text{Al}^{3+}\)), organic matter likely became tightly associated with newly formed mineral surfaces or precipitated mineral phases. Because these bonds are no longer primarily held together by easily exchangeable ions, removing the exchangeable cation pool did not drastically disrupt the carboxylate spectral environment.

Alternative Hypotheses & Experimental Verification

  1. Cation Saturation/Incomplete Extraction
    • Hypothesis: Weathering may have released a pool of structural \(\text{Ca}^{2+}\) or \(\text{Mg}^{2+}\) so large that the current extraction protocol could not completely deplete it, resulting in a deceptively small net spectral change.
    • Validation: We can rule this out by running a longer extraction time for the full sample set (the preliminary test only used a 30-minute extraction window).
  2. Physical Aggregation & Mass Transfer Limitations
    • Hypothesis: The rock powder treatment may promote physical aggregation, physically shielding reactive surfaces from interacting with the extraction solution.
    • Validation: We will explicitly test this physical stabilization mechanism via the upcoming sonication experiment.
